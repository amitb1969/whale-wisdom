      Reflections on 2025
      Having closed out 2025, it is worth reflecting on both the year past and what we see for the year ahead.
      In many respects, 2025 felt like an amplification of recent market dynamics: the perceived AI winners
      rallied higher, while ostensibly “cheap” stocks proved that they could get even cheaper if they were
      perceived to be AI losers.

      The Fund has benefitted from the strong performance of a number of AI winners. For example,
      AppLovin has been applying machine learning to improve its ad algorithms, and to great effect. The
      company’s Software Platform revenues and adjusted EBITDA on a last twelve month basis are up 72%
      and 89% year-over-year, respectively. In the 16 months since we first bought AppLovin, the stock has
      increased in value by more than 10x. We mapped out our bullish AppLovin thesis in our September
      2024 quarterly letter before the market appreciated the company’s ability to sustain its explosive
      growth.

      In our September 2025 quarterly letter we explained why we had taken positions in Alphabet and
      ASML. We highlighted how ASML is a monopoly in the semiconductor industry during an AI boom.
      Our writeup outlined the bear case arguments and explained our reasoning for why they were
      misguided. Other investors began to agree with our investment thesis, sending the stock from around
      €600 per share to north of €900 per share in the span of a quarter. Alphabet, where investor sentiment
      around AI had become unduly pessimistic, has also since delivered solid performance and contributed
      meaningfully to the Fund’s returns.

      In all of these examples of winning investments, the market quickly came around to our view, leading
      to a sharp re-rating in the stock price. Ideally, we are only aggressively buying and sizing up stocks just
      before they inflect, yet it is unreasonable to expect to perfectly time all our investments. While there
      was skill in identifying a gap between the financial performance we thought these businesses would
      achieve and the expectations of other investors, it was mostly lucky that the market began to agree with
      us imminently after we made those investments.

      While we do deep work in forming our high conviction views on the risk/reward of investments,
      unfortunately that conviction does not extend to the timing of when the market will agree with us for
      any given stock. Sometimes this convergence of opinions will already be underway when we begin
      purchasing the stock, which is the ideal scenario. However, the market often remains indifferent for
      extended periods, or worse still, allows specious narratives to drive the share price lower.

      We do not profess to have a crystal ball regarding market sentiment. It is a variable we have misjudged
      many times in the past. Instead, we remain comfortable committing capital to stocks where the
      discrepancy between price and value is wide, having the patience to wait for the market to revise its
      view while the business compounds its intrinsic value over time.

      Unfortunately, the strong contribution to the Fund from the abovementioned stocks has been offset by
      very weak performance in two of the Fund’s largest positions: Hemnet Group and PAR Technology
      Corporation. Both positions have more than halved in value peak to trough – a disappointing outcome.

      to brighten. Despite this, the stock’s performance during the quarter was weak, falling by -8%. PAR

      The investment case for PAR has strengthened materially in recent months, driven by what we view as

      contract with a mega tier-1 which we believe to be McDonald’s.

      That fact that McDonald’s, one of the most tech forward restaurants that has supported its own in-

      volumes. It suggests that even the largest, best-capitalised players are realising that the mission-critical
      layer of their tech stack – that is, the POS software that processes 100% of their revenue – is too
      complex and expensive to maintain internally, and is more effectively managed by a best-of-breed third
      party vendor like PAR.

      This dynamic directly counters the bear case that generative AI will allow companies to simply “vibe
      code” their own software solutions cheaply. We believe the opposite is true: when the POS software
      layer costs a mere 15-20 basis points of a restaurant’s average unit volumes but POS downtime results
      in operational paralysis, the asymmetry of risk demands enterprise-grade stability and support, not AI-
      produced improvisational code. McDonald’s potential capitulation on “building it themselves” would be
      the ultimate validation of PAR’s customer value proposition.

      Rather than being displaced by AI, we view PAR as a beneficiary. The fragmented nature of legacy
      restaurant IT – where the POS, loyalty program, and back-of-house systems sit in siloed databases –
      makes deploying AI challenging for most chains. PAR’s competitive advantage is its unified platform
      strategy, which stitches these modules together into a single data lake.

      Adobe (ADBE) – an AI loser that isn’t losing
      We utilised the recent market pessimism around AI losers to initiate a position in Adobe (ADBE) at 15x
      earnings, a valuation low that hasn’t been reached in over 13 years. While the bearish consensus view is
      that generative AI is an existential threat that will lead to reduced usage of Adobe’s creative suite, the
      business continues to print very healthy financial results, growing revenue at double digit rates and
      earnings at a mid-teens percentage rate. We see a world where Adobe is a beneficiary of the Jevons
      paradox: as AI collapses the cost of generating raw content, the demand for digital content will explode,
      exponentially increasing the demand for the tools required to edit, assemble, and manage that output.

      Our thesis is that while AI may commoditize creation, it entrenches the value of the workflow. Adobe
      can be thought of as the orchestrator of professional design workflows; whether an asset is born from a
      camera or a prompt, it almost invariably ends up in Photoshop or Premiere for final production. We are
      already seeing early evidence of this, with AI-integrated features driving higher retention and usage
      rather than displacement.

      Timee – the Japanese spot work monopolist
      We have established a position in Timee, the monopoly operator of Japan’s “spot work” (sukima baito)
      labour market, at a valuation of 21x earnings – an entry price we view as exceptional given we believe
      the company has runway for 20% annualised revenue growth and 25-30% per annum earnings growth
      over the next five years. Timee addresses Japan’s critical labour shortage by providing a marketplace
      that matches workers with immediate, short-term shifts.

      Our conviction was significantly bolstered following the capitulation of Timee’s most aggressive
      competitor: Mercari Hallo. In October 2025, Mercari announced that it would withdraw from the
      Japanese spot work market. This is after an unsuccessful attempt to win meaningful market share over
      an 18 month period, despite Mercari allowing employers to use its service for free (i.e., at a 0% take
      rate) compared to Timee’s 30% take rate.

      This retreat by a key competitor validates our view that Timee’s network is incredibly difficult to
      replicate. Scaling a spot work marketplace requires maintaining a delicate, hyper-local equilibrium
      between supply and demand. If the platform tilts too heavily towards workers, job fill rates remain high

      Floor & Decor Holdings (FND) – a stock that has been on our shopping list
      A category killer business model
      Founded in 2000, Floor & Decor (FND) is a retailer of hard surface flooring products such as wood,
      tiles, luxury vinyl plank, and related accessories. While this might not sound very exciting, the company
      has grown its comparable store sales at almost 11% per annum on average over the last 14 years, with
      revenue and adjusted EBITDA growing at 24% and 25% per annum, respectively, between 2011 and
      2024.

      It is not so much what FND sells, it is the manner in which it sells its mostly private label flooring
      products that lies at the core of what gives the company an advantage over competitors. FND is selling
      its merchandise in 75,000 square foot warehouse stores, compared to a mere 3,000 to 5,000 square
      feet of space that big box home improvement chains such as Home Depot and Lowes dedicate to selling
      hard surface flooring products.




      Source: Floor Covering Weekly. Inside a Floor & Decor store.

      By operating large-format warehouse stores, Floor & Decor can both merchandise far more flooring
      stock keeping units (SKUs) than peers, and, critically, hold those SKUs in job-ready quantities. FND
      carries ~2,350+ flooring SKUs versus ~630-680 at competing home improvement centres, with ~1,730
      flooring SKUs actually in stock – around 4x the in-stock depth of big box competitors.




      Source: FND 2022 Investor Day

      This inventory depth matters most to Pro customers – that is, professional flooring installers working
      to fixed timelines. Pros need to purchase the entire job quantity upfront, including overage, and from a
      single dye-lot to ensure visual consistency. When a flooring SKU is either partially available or out-of-
      stock it is operationally disruptive, forcing a special order with a one-to-two week wait time that leads
      to labour downtime, the postponement of job completion, and it delays payment for the Pro.

      By consistently stocking job-ready quantities of flooring SKUs, Floor & Decor enables Pros to complete
      jobs faster, keep crews productive, and accelerate cash collection. It’s no wonder that Floor & Decor has
      been a hit with Pro customers.

      Sales to Pro customers represent half of FND’s sales, up from 40% in early 2022. Floor & Decor is not
      so much a store as it is a reliable supply partner for the Pro customer. This over-indexation to the Pro is
      desirable, given that these customers transact at a significantly higher frequency than do-it-yourself
      (DIY) customers, reducing customer acquisition costs per transaction and underpinning robust
      customer unit economics.

      Pro customers’ affinity for purchasing from FND has also been aided by the company’s loyalty program.
      In early 2022, it was disclosed that 59% of active Pro customers were enrolled in Floor & Decor’s PRO
      Premier Rewards Program, and these Pros spend 3x more than Pros who aren’t enrolled in the loyalty
      program. FND’s loyalty program deepens engagement, incentivises repeat purchasing, and improves
      Pro customer retention.

      It is worth noting that FND follows an Everyday Low Price (EDLP) pricing strategy whereby, as the
      name suggests, the company offers consistently low prices year-round. This is in contrast to the
      sporadic, temporary promotions under a “high-low” pricing approach. There are a number of benefits
      that come from FND following an EDLP strategy:

         •   The stable prices under an EDLP regime builds trust with customers; these customers know
             that they’re getting a fair price from Floor & Decor without needing to haggle or wait for
             promotions.

         •   EDLP helps drive a self-reinforcing flywheel: consistently low prices lead to higher sales
             volumes, with these higher sales volumes enabling a further reduction in per unit costs via
             improved purchasing leverage with suppliers and fixed costs being spread over more units.
             Some portion of these cost savings are passed back to consumers in the form of lower prices,
             which in turn drives even more volumes. This virtuous retail cycle is a scale game and is difficult
             for would-be competitors to replicate.
         •   An EDLP approach is more operationally efficient than high-low pricing. If we consider the
             lumpy promotional cadence of a high-low pricing strategy, it results in demand spikes during
             discount periods that put immense pressure on the retailer’s supply chain (overtime labour in
             distribution centres and stores, expedited freight and other inefficiencies). It also makes
             inventory planning more difficult; missing the forecast with an inventory pre-buy could either
             result in stock-outs during the promotion (foregone sales) or excess inventory afterwards
             (higher markdown risk to clear this inventory, eroding gross margins). Lastly, high-low creates
             promotional complexity and higher marketing costs. FND’s EDLP approach avoids these issues
             that stem from high-low pricing, with potential savings that can again be passed back to
             consumers by way of lower prices.
         •   Pricing certainty helps Pros quote jobs accurately, and EDLP obviates the need to wait for
             promotions to start a job. Rather, EDLP encourages repeat purchasing from Pros where the
             reliability of inventory supply matters more than promotion-driven bargains.

      The power of a store rollout strategy
      The beauty of any store concept that resonates with the consumer is that it holds the potential for
      copies of that store to be rolled out across the country. Walmart and countless other retailers have done
      this spectacularly well and have highlighted the immense wealth generation that can result from well-
      executed store rollout strategies.

      As of Q3 2025, FND had 262 stores. The company has a longer term target of 500 stores, which was
      upped from the prior 400 store target a few years ago. It’s clear that the company still has a lot of
      runway before it reaches its 500 store target (and there is likely upside to this end-state store count via
      a potential expansion into the Canadian market).

      sale of a home is a catalyst for a flooring remodel, and thus a potential flooring purchase from Floor &

      Will we see lower mortgage rates?
      What is needed to reinvigorate existing home sales is lower interest rates, and more specifically, a
      decline in the 30 year fixed mortgage rate which has been stubbornly hovering around 6.3%.

      Lower mortgage rates are needed to thaw property transactions because many American homeowners
      have locked in 30 year fixed mortgages at significantly lower rates than current mortgage rates, making
      them reluctant to move and abandon their low-rate mortgage. Consider that nearly 30 million U.S.
      households, or 54% of primary mortgage holders, have mortgage rates at or below 4%.

      However, this lock-in effect is already beginning to fade. While the cohort of homeowners with sub-4%
      mortgages peaked at 65.1% of mortgages outstanding in early 2022, that figure has already retreated to
      51.5% (with sub-5% loans dropping from 85.6% to 68.6%).

      While the rate differential between written mortgages and current yields has created a massive
      disincentive for homeowners to move (and thus paralysing inventory and housing transactions), time is
      naturally dissolving this friction. There are life events called the “5 Ds” that can compel a home sale
      even in an elevated interest rate environment:

         •   Diapers (growing family needs more space)
         •   Diamonds (marriage/household formation)
         •   Divorce (household dissolution)
         •   Death (estate sales)
         •   Displacement (job relocation)

      While life events are gradually eroding the mortgage lock-in effect, this is a slow process. Rather, the
      most immediate catalyst for restoring housing transaction velocity is the need for a reduction in the 30-
      year fixed mortgage rate.

      We would point out that the Federal Reserve has been cutting interest rates, which could filter through
      to a reduction in U.S. mortgage rates. This already appears to be underway, with a circa 100 basis point
      reduction in 30-year fixed mortgage rates over the last year.

      However, it is important to acknowledge that a lower Fed Funds Rate does not automatically translate
      into lower 30-year mortgage rates. The Federal Reserve can push down the short end of the curve (i.e.,
      yields on 2-year Treasurys) but the 30-year mortgage rate is priced off the long end of the curve (10-
      year Treasurys, to be specific). Mortgage rates are thus influenced by long-term inflation expectations,
      Treasury supply (i.e., government debt issuance), and mortgage-specific risk premia demanded by MBS
      investors.

      In a rudimentary way, we can conceptualise how a 30-year fixed mortgage is priced with the following
      formula:

                            Mortgage Rate = 10-Year Treasury Yield + Mortgage Spread

      When interest rate volatility is high, the spread (i.e., the extra yield) that investors demand to buy
      mortgage-backed securities (MBS) instead of safe Treasurys increases.

      This is primarily driven by negative convexity: because every mortgage contains an embedded call
      option that creates prepayment risk (i.e., the borrower’s right to refinance when interest rates decline),
      an MBS investor’s upside is capped while their downside remains fully exposed. In volatile markets, the
      cost to hedge this short option position escalates, forcing investors to demand a wider spread to
      compensate them for taking on this asymmetric risk.

      market (specifically, how expensive it is to buy options on Treasurys) – has been elevated in recent
      years. It is now beginning to subside.

      Operating leverage working in FND’s favour
      Perhaps the most potent driver of a FND earnings rebound is the company’s operating leverage.
      Unfortunately for FND, the last few years have instead seen operating deleverage. Given the tough
      macroenvironment, FND’s comparable store sales declined by around -7% in both FY23 and FY24, with
      FY25 likely to be down by a low-single-digit percentage rate. When we consider the company’s fixed
      cost base – comprised of leases, core store staffing, and corporate overhead – this has led to a
      disproportionately large reduction in FND’s earnings (management has previously stated that 55% of
      FND’s store costs are fixed and 45% are variable).

      income achieved in FY22, and adjusted operating margins have gone from 10-11% in FY21-FY22 to
